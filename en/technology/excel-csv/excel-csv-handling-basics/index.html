CSV Handling in Excel: Encoding, Leading Zeros, and Date Conversion

Import CSV files safely in Excel and prevent encoding problems, lost leading zeros, long-number changes, and automatic date conversion.

Published: 2026-07-22Last updated: 2026-07-22
In this guide
  1. Fundamentals
  2. Syntax
  3. Steps
  4. Business examples
  5. Common errors

Fundamentals

CSV represents tables as text separated by delimiters and does not preserve Excel formatting or formulas. Opening by double-click can trigger automatic type conversion.

Syntax

安全な読込
データ → テキストまたはCSVから → 文字コード確認 → 各列のデータ型を指定

Steps

  1. Preserve a copy of the original CSV.
  2. Import through Excel Get Data.
  3. Confirm encoding and delimiter.
  4. Set postal codes, employee IDs, and product codes to text.
  5. Verify row count, column count, first and last rows, and totals before saving.

Business examples

  • preserving leading zeros in postal codes
  • preventing scientific notation for long IDs
  • importing Japanese UTF-8 CSV data
  • keeping date-like text in its original representation

Common errors

  • double-clicking and overwriting the original
  • assuming CSV preserves multiple sheets or formulas
  • trusting displayed values without checking source text
  • saving corrupted text into another format
Key point

After building the formula, test zero values, blanks, duplicates, and boundary cases.

Frequently asked questions

Where do I enter the formula?

Select the result cell and enter the formula beginning with an equals sign. Then verify the highlighted references and the formula bar.

What if the result does not update?

Check automatic calculation, whether referenced cells are numeric, and whether external links or filters affect the result.

Can I copy the formula to other rows?

Yes, but verify that relative and absolute references behave as intended before filling the formula down.
